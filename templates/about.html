What is "Creative Generator"?

Back to top

This is the Capstone Project created during my senior year at Minerva.

Given the open question “How can machine learning facilitate creative thinking - the process of generating new ideas?”, the goal of this project is to illustrate how a model could be trained to recognize ways in which words or concepts are connected and generate suggestions based on a string of input to foster the process of brainstorming, generating creative ideas and solving problems.

This tool brings about an interesting application of AI in creative study, because this emulates the technique proven to be an indicator of creative potential called divergent thinking. The goal of divergent thinking is to generate many different ideas about a topic in a short period of time by breaking a topic down into its various component parts in order to gain insight about the various aspects of the topic. Divergent thinking typically occurs in a spontaneous, free-flowing manner, such that the ideas are generated in a random, unorganized fashion. This process usually takes a long time, resources and efforts because human’s imagination is limited. Therefore the tool will help a lot in this step. After that, following divergent thinking, the ideas and information will be organized by users using convergent thinking; i.e., putting the various ideas back together in some organized, structured way.

Some features:

  • Users can query a prompt and receive suggestions without logging in
  • Users can manage their notes, past queries and favourite suggestions by creating an account

What is the technology behind "Creative Generator"?

Back to top

The front-end of this website is built by utilizing a sample from getbootstrap.com and the back-end is built using the Flask micro framework. The backbone of this tool is a machine learning model that takes input as a string and return the predictions for each of the key word in that string, if that word is presented in the database.

What happens when you insert a prompt into the search box?

  1. The prompt would be split into different keywords that are available in our database.
  2. For each keyword, the machine would look for 50 words that are close to it semantically. The model determines the semantic distances between words by evaluating how frequent they appear together from a Google News dataset.
  3. Among these 50 results for each key word, 10 would be chosen randomly by the machine. This therefore yield 10 different things at different times for the same keyword.
  4. The tool would then create 10 random combination of words that correspond to each original keyword
  5. These combinations would then be turned into queries that are fed into external image search engines such as Google Image, Pinterest, Giphy, ...

What is the literature behind the idea?

Back to top

A literature review on creativity especially focusing on the working theories around creative problem solving provides justification as to why this project would fill in an existing gap by providing a tool needed to facilitate creative problem solving.

A/ Definition of creativity

Image by Jason Theodor

Similar to “concept”, the definition of creativity is not yet finalized by researchers in the field. One distinction that I consider to be important is the difference between creativity within specific domains and creativity as a separate, stand-alone process applied within different domains. The dominant definition of creativity in the field mostly focused on the generic case.

Another distinction is between “high creativity” and “ordinary creativity”. High creativity is viewed as “the ability to produce new knowledge” (Dacey & Lennon, 2000). This definition is considered to be limiting in the sense that it can only be studied in extraordinarily talented people, and may be of less relevance when we want to focus on the education process of all people. On the other hand, ordinary creativity “involves being imaginative, going beyond the obvious, being aware of one’s own unconventionality, being original in some way. It is not necessarily linked with a product-outcome”

On the other hand, thinking about creativity through an outcome-oriented lense, Mumford concluded that “Creativity involves the production of novel, useful products” (Mumford, 2003). In this sense, there is a distinguishment that can be made between creative arts and creative problem solving because each corresponds to a specific type of product. "Creative generator" could prove to be useful in both cases, but is more tailored to facilitate creative problem solving . The core to this aspect of creativity - creative problem solving is Torrance’s definition of creativity as the process of understanding a problem, searching for possible solutions, generating hypotheses, testing and evaluating and finally communicating the results to others. (Torrance, 1969). In other word, it involves two main processes: generating original ideas and then recombining them or seeing new relationships among old ideas. This is the key piece that the development of my tool is based on - the divergent and convergent processes.

B/ Creativity and cognition

Cognition study has classified creativity as an aspect of intelligence (Binet & Henri, 1896), as a mainly unconscious process (Poincare, 1913, Freud, 1957) and as a problem-solving capacity (Wallas, 1926).

Two major lines of creativity investigation in the latter half of the 20th century are named psychometrics and experimental psychodynamics. Psychometrics basically focused on divergent thinking as an indicator of creative potential. This was pioneered by Guilford who created a tool to measure the level of “divergent production” (Guilford, 1967). A more recent study by Mark A. Runco also showed that even though divergent thinking does not guarantee actual creative achievement, tests of divergent thinking are reliable and reasonably valid predictors of certain performance criteria. Psychodynamics mostly focused on the relationship between personality, perception and creativity. The studies showed that creative person has certain characteristics which are: talent to make alternative views of reality, courage to go against tradition and special emotion towards the work of creation (Schoon, 1992, Anderson & Rhyammer, 1998). Recent researches emphasized on the understanding of creative mind in terms of intelligence (Gardner, 1993).

C/ Creative Problem Solving

There are worked-out theories about creative problem solving that can shed light on the meaning and usefulness of this project. The two main theories of problem solving are the search in a problem space approach developed by Newell and Simon and the problem representation approach developed by Gestalt psychologists.

The search in a problem space approach

In the book Human Problem Solving, Newell and Simon outlined the problem space theory of problem solving: people solve problems by continuously searching in a problem space, which consists of “the initial state, the goal state and all possible states in between”. Most of the time, the problem spaces become very large that we need to rely on either domain knowledge to make decisions at each step, or the power of computer to explore all the possibilities. Also, with the help of certain heuristics, the search in the space becomes much more effective. Some typical heuristics are: repeat-state avoidance (avoiding going back to a previous state) and hill-climbing (taking the step that associates with the shortest distance between the current state to the goal state). (Newell and Simon, 1972). This theory of problem solving, however, is more suitable for non-creative problem solving than creative problem solving because it requires a pre-defined goal state (a correct answer) and in each step the solver would try to move from the initial state, through all possible states to get closer to the goal state. In creative problem solving, this goal-state is difficult to define, which makes it hard to evaluate what would be the best next step.


The problem representation approach

On the other hand, the problem representation approach developed by the Gestalt theorists is more relevant to creative problem solving because it is about how solving a problem involves a “reorganization or reconstruction of the representation of the problem in the mind”, which leads to a flash of “insight experiences” that is essential for coming up with a solution. This “insight” is experienced suddenly and unpredictably, and is also widely known as the “aha!” moment in problem solving (Finke et al., 1992; Smith, 1995a). The approach of discovering a new or different way to frame a problem helps to mitigate the existing barriers to creative problem solving, one of which is called the functional fixedness. Functional fixedness is basically a cognitive bias that hinders human from realizing that objects can be used for multiple purposes other than what they are originally designed for. (Duncker, 1945). This is illustrated in some problems such as the Maier’s two string problem (Maier, 1930) and the candle problem (Duncker, 1945). In the paper “Limitation of the current stock of ideas about problem solving”, Newell pointed out that there is a limit in the tool or method created to facilitate the shift of problem representation. Most of the time, people get stuck or even don’t know where to start: “with no representation there is no possibility of manipulation and no way of making progress, thus the initial lost period is devoted to finding a representation”. (Newell, 1965). Strongly related to the goal of my project, this period of finding a representation could be made more effective with the help of computer. The main task for machine is to generate as much information that is related to the given prompt or problem as possible so that human can find out a way to reframe the problem to come up with a creative solution.
